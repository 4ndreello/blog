# Digital Garden Philosophy

A digital garden is a collection of imperfect, evolving notes published on the web. Unlike a blog, where posts are finished and chronological, a garden is **topological** and **ever-growing**.

## Seeds, plants, and evergreens

Notes in a garden are often classified by maturity:

- **Seedlings** — rough ideas, barely formed.
- **Plants** — developed thoughts with some structure.
- **Evergreens** — polished, reference-quality notes that are updated over time.

## Interconnectedness

The power of a garden comes from links. Every note should reference related ideas, creating a web of knowledge rather than a linear stream. This mirrors how human memory works: associative, non-linear, and context-dependent.

## Learning in public

Publishing unfinished work feels vulnerable, but it is also generous. It allows others to learn alongside you, correct mistakes, and discover connections you missed. The garden is a conversation, not a monologue.

## My approach

This site is my personal garden. I write about infrastructure, programming, and ideas I am still digesting. Some notes are weeks old; others are years old and regularly pruned. Treat it as a workspace, not a museum.
